Paleo Explore

is an app designed to let you explore archaeological evidence from the Palaeolithic period (7 million years ago to 30 thousande years ago) at a similar rate to which they occured. You can also choose to explore the evidence by walking a to scale distance between the sites of evidence.

tchadensis

The oldest hominin!


We only have a skull


Did they live in the trees? We don't know but we can see from the skull that they may have had a more upright posture because the space where the foreman magnum meets the skull is lower down (on the underside of the skull) than in other primates.

Back

This is a bonobo, but as we only have one Sahelanthropus tchadensis skull we have to guess what they would have looked like. We often use our closest living species, like the bonobo, as a model for what our ancestors would look like. They wouldn't be a bonobo but they might be similar

Perhaps Sahelanthropus spent more time upright to hold objects like the Bonobos do

In 1999 Whitten found that seperate Chimpanzee groups collected ants (like above) in different ways, without ecological reasons. This suggests they have "culture".


As we also have culture it suggests we share this trait with all our shared ancestor and all previous hominins

Back

In 1987 a man called Blumenschine travelled round the Serengeti National Park and noted down the amount of meat available for scavengers. He found that there was just enough for a hominin to live on. However, in the past sabre tooth tigers may have meant there were far more unfinished carcasses to utilise as their teeth stop them from eating all the available nutrition of their kill.


The debate over whether Australopithecus afarensis was a hunter of a scavenger still rages.

Back

Dated to 3.4 million years ago these footprints were captured in rapidly drying volcanic ash. They clearly demonstrate that Australopithecus afarensis, the hominin which inhabited the area at the time walked upright and walked bipedally. There was no difference in the way these hominins walked and people today.

Back

Why do we have an upright posture?


With the evidence from autralopith pelvic bones and the laotoli footprints providing evidence australopiths were bipedal we're forced to ask why they are upright.


- To hold tools and offspring?

- To see over long grass?

- To reach fruit on bushes?

- To regulate heat?


Whilst we have no way to be sure the increased aridity of the period definitely had a roll to play in pushing our ancestors out of the rainforest

Back

In the 1960s a startling discovery was made: the most complete ancient hominin skeleton of the time. It was dubbed Lucy because the archaeologists had been listening to the song "Lucy in the Sky with Diamonds" at the time they found it. It was a well preserved Australopithecus afarensis skeleton. Many of its proportions were still ape like: there was a flared rib cage to make room for a big gut, a curved pelvis and longer arms. However the pelvis was less curved than modern day apes suggesting adaptations for more bipedalism

Back

For many years it was thought that Australopithecus afarensis did not use stone tools, but in 2002 Panger firmly argued that there was no morphological reason why they wouldn't. They had the hand that could do it, and archaeologists would be hard pushed to know if a random, unworked stone, had been used as a tool. It would just look like a stone to any archaeologist!

In 2010 McPherron and colleagues announced the oldest evidence of stone tool use: cut marks on a bone dating to 3.4 to 3.2 million years ago

Back

Paranthropus boisei had the most pronounced cheek arches of any ancient hominin. It also had a pronounced zigomatic arch (the crest along the top of the skull) which the temporal muscles attached to.


Put your fingers on either side of your forehead and clench your teeth, you should feel your temporal muscles working.


These hominins would have had temporal muscles many times the size of ours today, They would have had incredibly strong jaws

Back

Plants can take in different quantities of different types of carbon. Parts of the body, like tooth enamel then use these specific types of carbon. By studying Paranthropus boisei tooth enamel Cerling and colleagues (2011) found that boisei was eating far more plants which absorb Carbon4. These tend to be low nutrition tough grasses. The huge chewing power of the boisei skull could be an adaptation for eating grass

Back

Paranthropus robustus was a hominin that has been found in South Africa. It has distinctively wide cheek bones and a Zigomatic arch (the crest along the top of the skull) to hold large temporal muscles to work a very powerful jaw. Its teeth were much larger than any of the australopiths but otherwise their bodies appear similar.

Back

By studying the microwear on the teeth of ancient hominins palaeanthro-pologists can discover what the hominins ate. In 2011 Ungar and Sponheimer found that Paranthropus Robustus had teeth worn in the most variedly complex way. This could suggest a diet using more marginal and brittle foods unlike other hominins

This could by why their jaws and teeth needed to be so big. To deal with a harsher environment

Back

Backwell and d'Errico in 2001 studied bones found at sites with Paranthropus robustus, Swartkrans and Sterkfontein, like the image above, and compared the wear marks they found to bones they used to dig up underground tubers or termites. They found the wear patterns looked like the bone tools they used to dig for termites. It appears that Paranthropus robustus was using bone tools.

Back

Homo habilis was the first fossil hominin to be found with tools, which is why they were classed into the genus Homo.


These tools, known as Oldowan or Mode 1 or Chopper tools were simply worked by a process called Knapping:

It is now believed that getting the sharp flakes may have been the real purpose of knapping

Back

In 1984 an academic called Potts suggested that the distribution of stones at habilis sites suggested Stone Caching. The hominins were leaving collections of stones (worked and unworked) at strategic sites which would then be used when needed for butchery.


This could represent a leap in strategic landscape use and greater intelligence

Back

Gathering in large groups and collecting highly sort after meat resources has its risks. Rose & Marshall (1996) believe that Homo habilis would have gathered in larger groups to protect themselves instigating greater sociallity potentially leading to a need for greater brain size

This doesn't always work as this habilis tibia which has been gnawed on by crocodiles attests.


Many habilis remains have puncture marks from the teeth of big cats too

Back

A site at the lakeside of Lake Turkana in Kenya shows extensive utilisation of marine resources, such as catfish and turtles. Suggesting greater intelligence in the habilis species that used the site. Marine foods are high in fatty acids that are great for the brain suggesting they may have allowed the greater brain sizes reaches by Homo habilis

Back

In the 1950s Louis Leakey found the first evidence of early stone tools and a hominin with a larger cranial capacity which he dubbed Homo habilis, the handy human. Since then more evidence labelled homo habilis has emerged but often with very divergent cranial capacity and body parts which still appear small and ape like.

Back

With Homo erectus we see the first evidence of tools worked bimodally. They are often called acheulian handaxes.They are knapped on both sides of the tool which many consider as a break through in cognition as it potentially demonstrates a understanding of symmetry and aesthetic beauty. Brain scans indicate more areas of the brain are used when modern stone tool makers knapp handaxes

Back

The evolution of Homo erectus, or in its earliest forms sometimes called Homo ergaster, marks a dramatic change in body shape, forcing a decrease in gut size and different energy needs

Back

Humans are amazing at regulating their temperature. This is because of their shape, their abundant sweat glands and their hairless bodies. It makes them very good endurance runners. Homo erectus may have evolved its more human like shape to be better hunt prey.

Back

The increase in size between habilis and ergaster was such a large increase it would have imposed large energy demads, esoecially to lactating females, this could have necessitated males aiding in provisioning females and more monogamous social grouping

Dmanisi only has very simole tools in the early layers where there are also hominin fossils. There is a larger gap and then more complex hand axes. 1.7 million years ago there was a warmer period which would have created a similar savannah like environment from Africa and across much of Asia allowing the migration of many prey animals and perhaps the hominins that preyed upon them

This could have been because of poor stone available in the area and the abundance of bamboo which can be sharpened to a very good cutting edge. This seems more sensible than that chinese Homo erectus was cognitively unable
